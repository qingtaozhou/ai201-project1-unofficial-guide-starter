Blog Post: "Surviving Georgia Tech Master's Program: Course Selection & Time Management"
Author: Georgia Tech OMSA + CS Master's Student
Date: April 2026

Excerpt (Full Post):

I'm finishing my second semester of a CS Master's degree at Georgia Tech. I also did a summer internship and worked part-time last semester. Here's what I've learned about course selection and surviving the workload.

**The Mistake I Almost Made:**

Going into my first semester, I signed up for:
- CS 7641 (ML)
- CS 6505 (Algorithms)
- CS 6291 (Computer Vision)

That's 36 credit hours of graduate-level courses. I was warned by an advisor: "That's too much. You'll burn out."

I ignored them because I wanted to graduate fast. By week 3, I realized I was drowning. I dropped CS 6505 and added it for spring. Best decision I made.

**What I Learned About Workload:**

Graduate CS courses are NOT like undergrad. Here's the reality:

- Each course needs 12-15 hours/week of outside work
- "Lab" courses (CS 7641, CS 7646) require programming from scratch, not guided assignments
- Theory courses (CS 6505, CS 6340) require proof-writing and mathematical thinking
- There's minimal hand-holding compared to undergrad

**Smart Course Combinations (18 credit hours = 3 courses):**

GOOD combo:
- CS 7641 (ML, heavy) + CS 6340 (AI, medium) + CS 6300 (SWE, lighter coding)
→ Balances math-heavy with implementation

BAD combo:
- CS 6505 (theory) + CS 7641 (ML, theory-heavy) + CS 7646 (project-heavy)
→ All demanding, all different skill sets needed simultaneously

MEDIUM combo:
- CS 7641 (ML) + CS 6291 (Vision) + CS 6404 (Parallel Computing)
→ Heavy but coherent (all systems/ML track)

**Time Management Reality Check:**

I tracked my hours last semester:
- 7641: 14 hrs/week (exams compress it to 20 hrs during exam weeks)
- 6340: 11 hrs/week
- 6300: 8 hrs/week (lighter)

That's 33 hours PLUS attending class (3 hrs). Plus I worked 10 hrs/week part-time.

By the end: I got A's in 7641 and 6340, but I was exhausted. Don't be me.

**What Professors DON'T Tell You:**

1. Most professors assume you have research experience (you don't)
2. The homework is often harder than what they present in lectures
3. There's no "easy" grade — everyone has to earn it
4. Curves exist but don't count on them
5. Office hours are 80% PhD students, 20% Master's students (you'll feel out of place)

**Courses I Recommend vs. Avoid:**

TAKE:
- CS 7641 (ML) — if you want an ML career, non-negotiable
- CS 6340 (AI) — great breadth, well-taught
- CS 6200 (OS) — solid systems foundation

AVOID unless committed:
- CS 6505 (Algorithms) — only if theory matters for your goals
- CS 7646 (Trading) — only if you're interested in finance
- CS 6476 (Computer Vision) — very project-heavy, time sink

SKIP:
- Courses without clear relevance to your goal (you don't have time for exploration)

**My Recommendation for Time-Strapped Students:**

Take 2 hard courses + 1 medium course per semester.

Hard: 7641, 6505, 6476 (project-heavy vision)
Medium: 6340, 6200, 6291, 6300
Light: 6100 (Security), 6001 (Bio)

This way you make progress but don't burn out.

**One More Thing:**

The grad program is NOT like undergrad where you can recover with a last-minute grind. Concepts build incrementally. If you fall behind in week 2 of 7641, you'll be lost by midterm. Start assignments early. Attend lectures. Don't skip.
