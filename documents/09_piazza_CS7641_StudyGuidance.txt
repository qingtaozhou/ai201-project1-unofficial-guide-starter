STUDENT QUESTION:
On the exam, will we be given formulas or expected to derive them? How much memorization is there?

---

INSTRUCTOR RESPONSE (Prof. Isbell):

Great question. The short answer: Memorization is the WRONG approach. The long answer follows.

**What you will have on the exam:**
- Formula sheet: Yes, one page (both sides). Includes matrix operations, probability distributions, some common ML formulas.
- Textbook: No, closed book.
- Notes: No.

**What that means:**
Since you get a formula sheet, I'm NOT testing whether you memorized formulas. I'm testing whether you can:
1. Recognize which algorithm/technique applies to a problem
2. Apply it correctly
3. Explain why it works
4. Discuss trade-offs and assumptions

**Example:**
"Here's a dataset. A classmate suggests using SVM. Do you agree? Why or why not? What's an alternative approach?"

The answer isn't a formula. It's your reasoning.

**How to prepare:**
- Learn the INTUITION behind each algorithm (not the math)
- Practice on problems you haven't seen before
- Be able to explain algorithm choices to someone else
- Understand failure modes (when does logistic regression break?)

**Math level:**
You need to understand linear algebra and calculus conceptually. You don't need to prove theorems. Example: knowing that gradient descent moves opposite the gradient and why that works. NOT: deriving the proof that gradient descent converges.

**Common mistakes students make:**
1. They memorize the algorithm but can't apply it to a new problem
2. They avoid the math entirely and just focus on code
3. They don't think about when/why an algorithm fails

**My advice:**
If you can explain an algorithm to a smart friend who's not in this class, you understand it enough for the exam. If you can only recite it, you're not ready.

The homework is designed to teach understanding, not test memorization. Use it that way.
